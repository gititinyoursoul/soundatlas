# Birth of Hip-Hop Route Brief

Source basis: `docs/mvp-concept.md`

This is a lightweight pre-dossier brief. It frames the route idea before source
research, final event selection, connection design, or seed transfer.

## Route Idea

Birth of Hip-Hop follows how Bronx party culture, DJ technique, community
spaces, and media circulation helped turn hip-hop into a recognizable cultural
form between roughly 1970 and 1985.

The route should keep the MVP focused on the first vertical slice: **Birth of
Hip-Hop: Bronx 1970-1985**. It should make the app's three axes visible:
place, time, and sound/culture.

## Why This Route Matters

This route is the clearest first demonstration of the SoundAtlas premise. It
connects local places and social conditions to changing musical practice:
soundsystem influence, funk breaks, block parties, DJing, MCing, graffiti,
early rap records, and film or radio circulation.

The route should help users understand hip-hop as a developing network of
practices, communities, places, and media, not as a single isolated origin
moment.

## Initial Question

How did local Bronx party culture become a wider hip-hop cultural form between
roughly 1970 and 1985?

## Thesis Hypothesis

Hip-hop emerged through DJ practice, community spaces, urban conditions, and
media circulation. The route should test that argument against sources rather
than present any one place, party, artist, or record as the whole origin story.

## Research Targets

- Caribbean and Jamaican soundsystem influence in New York.
- Bronx DJ culture, funk breaks, block parties, parks, schools, and community
  centers.
- DJ technique, MCing, dance, graffiti, and the relationships among hip-hop's
  early cultural practices.
- Urban conditions in the Bronx, including how to frame 1977 without turning
  context into unsupported causality.
- Early rap records and the move from local performance culture into recorded
  circulation.
- Downtown club and art-scene crossover, film, radio, television, and other
  media paths that widened hip-hop's audience.
- Editorial sources, oral histories, archives, documentaries, and media leads
  that can support cautious event framing.

## Candidate Anchors

These anchors are research prompts only. They are not final seed events, final
route sequence, or source-vetted inclusions.

- Caribbean soundsystem influence and Bronx party practice.
- 1520 Sedgwick Avenue and Kool Herc's back-to-school jam as a symbolic origin
  story to review carefully.
- Bronx block parties, parks, schools, and community centers spreading DJ
  techniques.
- Grandmaster Flash and the refinement of DJ technique.
- The 1977 New York City blackout as urban context, with causality handled
  cautiously.
- "Rapper's Delight" and rap's visibility on record.
- Hip-hop reaching downtown clubs and the art scene.
- *Wild Style* and film documentation of early hip-hop culture.
- Early 1980s film, television, radio, and record circulation that helped
  hip-hop spread beyond New York.

## Known Risks

- Overstating a single birthplace, first event, or sole origin point.
- Treating contested origin stories as settled fact.
- Collapsing distinct Black, Latino, Caribbean, and Bronx community roles into
  a flat chronology.
- Presenting the 1977 blackout as a direct cause without source support.
- Misstating dates, places, active years, venue roles, or geocoded locations.
- Adding media or image links before rights, availability, and attribution
  review.
