This MVP edit is a fresh research dossier. It intentionally does not derive
from, audit, summarize, or revise
`docs/content/routes/birth-of-hip-hop/research-dossier.md`.

Here, "MVP edit" means a route-facing research pass for the current SoundAtlas
MVP after the numeric event, place, and connection targets were removed from
`docs/mvp-concept.md`. The purpose is to widen the possible research directions
while keeping the work tied to the MVP product promise: a map-first exploration
of New York music history across place, time, and sound/culture.

Candidate events and connections below are not final seed selections.

## Route Working Frame

- Working title: Birth of Hip-Hop
- Stable route ID: `birth-of-hip-hop`
- Geography: New York-centered, with the Bronx as the first research center and
  Harlem, Manhattan, other boroughs, regional industry nodes, and media
  circulation considered when they clarify the route argument.
- Time range: roughly 1970-1985, with earlier context allowed when it explains
  the conditions, communities, records, technology, or migration patterns that
  shaped the route.
- Current MVP fit: the first vertical slice for SoundAtlas, intended to test
  how a route can make music history explorable through map, timeline,
  inspector text, sources, media, and visible relationships.
- Central question: How did local Bronx party culture become a wider hip-hop
  cultural form between roughly 1970 and 1985?
- Draft thesis: Hip-hop developed through overlapping practices, communities,
  technologies, spaces, urban conditions, and media circuits. The route should
  explore those relationships without reducing the story to a single origin
  point, artist, place, record, or crisis.

Research stance:

This dossier should keep more doors open than a seed-transfer plan. It may
include candidate material that later becomes an event, route context, a place
note, a connection, a source lead, or a future route question.

Draft narrative phases:

1. Conditions before the named origin story: migration, records, sound systems,
   local parties, youth culture, public space, and Bronx urban conditions.
2. Practices becoming culture: DJing, MCing, dance, graffiti, crews, party
   promotion, sound equipment, and repeated neighborhood events.
3. Local practice meeting institutions: clubs, labels, radio, press, downtown
   art spaces, film, television, and museums or archives.
4. Circulation and memory: how early hip-hop moved through records, images,
   films, oral histories, anniversary narratives, and contested origin stories.

## Risks And Open Claims

| Claim or risk | Affected route content | Current wording guidance | Review need |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Sole birthplace framing | Sedgwick, route thesis, public copy | Use "associated with", "often cited", "symbolic origin story", or explain contested language. | Source comparison and editorial review. |
| One-person origin framing | Route thesis, DJs, Sedgwick | Present named figures as central nodes in a broader culture. | Compare oral histories and secondary sources. |
| Community flattening | Whole route | Distinguish Black, Latino, Caribbean, youth, neighborhood, and practitioner roles where sources support it. | Source diversity and sensitivity review. |
| Gender omission | Artists, organizers, labels, crews | Ask where organizing, promotion, production, performance, and writing labor involved women. | Targeted source review. |
